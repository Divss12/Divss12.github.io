An Exploration in Pathfinding Algorithms and Embedded Programming in Robotics: Multi-Disciplinary Project

March 2022 | Divyansh Bhutra

 

What does it take for a robotic car to see, move around and think? And how are these abilities used in conjunction to navigate an arena while avoiding obstacles? In this blog post, I will walk you through a project where I programmed a robotic car to do these things: from embedded programming allowing a microprocessor board to take the input from infrared sensors and facilitate movement using motors, to using a pathfinding algorithm to navigate the arena.

The Problem

We were provided with a robot car with a STMicroelectronics Board, motors, IR distance sensors, a RaspberryPi and a camera (some assembly of the components was required.) The goal? To have the robot navigate an arena full of obstacles, scanning images with its camera and reporting those images back to our android app. The second goal was a speed test: to have the robot go around a large obstacle and make it back to its starting location. Both competitions tested for speed and for accuracy.

STM Part

The first order of business was to assemble the robot. The RaspberryPi, infrared sensors and camera were screwed onto the robot. We soldered the wires required to ensure they wouldn’t fall off, and then we got to programming. First, we established a way for the RaspberryPi to communicate with the STM board using UART and with the android app using Bluetooth. Then we worked with the team to come up with a protocol to pass messages between these 3 components. Then we programmed a set of discrete movements the robot could take, which chained together would be how the robot would navigate the arena. We also configured the IR sensor as a failsafe to ensure the robot would stop if it’s about to crash into an obstacle.

Pathfinding Algorithm

As we would know the locations of the obstacles beforehand, we simply had to devise an algorithm for the robot to navigate the entire arena as quick as possible. To do this, we broke down the problem into graph-search problem. By dividing the arena into a 20x20 grid in which the robot could be in any square in 4 possible orientations (North, South, East, West); we can create a graph with 1600 nodes. Edges between these nodes represent the different movements our robot could take changing its orientation and location. After reducing the problem to a known problem (graph search), we were able to simply apply Dijkstra’s algorithm and have the robot find its path around the obstacles.
